MAINTAINING WELL BEING : PART 1

    • Cultivating positive emotions

  • Cultivating positive emotions is an important aspect of your well being. Research suggests that negative emotions and positive emotions are not just polar opposites. Similarly, most people do not consider "I am not unhappy" to mean the same as "I am happy".


    Reducing high intensity or high frequency negative emotions is a critical step in reducing your distress. However cultivating positive emotions can help in further enhancing your well being and strengthening your capacity to bounce back from stress.


    Here are a few simple pointers for cultivating positive emotions

More food for thought on cultivate positive emotions

  • Hedonism is a way of thinking and behavior, with an aim to experience maximum pleasurable sensations and minimizing any kind of pain. It relates with a lifestyle organized around things that give an immediate sense of sensory pleasure, excitement or relief (the nature of these pleasurable activities vary from person to person).

    Engaging in pleasure -oriented activities can give a temporary boost to your well being, but often this boost is short lasting. This means that one is required to engage in such activities again and again in order to feel good.
    There is nothing abnormal in itself about engaging in short lasting sensory pleasurable activities, but it could be problematic in a few situations as mentioned below:


    • a) When these are carried out very often or when these come in ways that interfere with your doing other activities- other activities that do not give you immediate pleasure but are useful for you in the longer run

    b) When these pleasure-oriented activities are harmful for your health (e.g. smoking, repeatedly going for a bar of sweet to feel good)

    c) Sometimes, frequently engaging in such activities can actually be a marker of feeling distressed.


    There are times when we try to cope with our negative feelings (e.g. sadness, hurt, anxiety or boredom) by seeking out pleasure - oriented activities to get a quick relief/to feel better. This can help lift our mood to an extent. But we need to simultaneously figure out how to deal with the underlying distress and find other methods of coping too- which have longer term benefits.


    Therefore we need to balance these with meaning-oriented activities that can enhance our overall well being. These are focused on experience of meaning, living in alignment with ones deepest values and evolving as a person and are not motivated by the need to experience immediate sense of pleasure or relief. These could include behaviours and actions that provide a sense of deep connection with ones inner self, with others, and provide us a chance to manifest the best in us. The exact nature of meaning-oriented activities, of course, varies from person to person depending on peoples temperament, preferences, skills and values (e.g. spiritual pursuits, volunteering, meaningful discussions, doing something creative and constructive, listening to someone and supporting them, being with nature etc.) Exploring what kinds of activities may help you express your deepest values and investing time and efforts in such activities can add to your sense of fulfillment in life.

On the other hand, formal volunteering is providing help for a cause/for a group of people through enrolling as a volunteer in an organization (e.g. an NGO).

    MAINTAINING WELL BEING: PART 2

      • Emotional well being

      Well being is often seen as experiencing more of positive emotions and less of negative emotions. We discussed about cultivating positive emotions in the previous sections.


      We need to remember that negative emotions also have their own value and utility. Negative emotions point toward what needs to be managed/corrected/dealt with. Moreover, expecting that one should 'never' feel negative emotions/ be 'stress-free all the time' can in itself be stressful and unrealistic!


      Although there is continuing debate and research on what is an optimal balance between positive and negative emotions, the ratios wherein negative emotions dominate the positive emotions are likely to be problematic, in most contexts.


      Ratios wherein positive emotions outweigh the negative ones (e.g. 3: 1 and 5: 1) have been suggested as beneficial by researchers in this area, though these should be taken tentatively rather than as any fixed magical figures. Moreover, the optimum ratio in one situation may not be the best/most ideal one in another situation and we normally tend to operate within a range rather than at fixed points. You would know your positivity ratio if you take the emotional well being test on at least two occasions.


      As you will realize, improving the positivity ratio can involve cultivating positive emotions as well as working to decrease the frequency with which we experience negative emotions. If you think that you are experience negative emotions more frequently, you may find it useful to go over the sections on thinking errors, self compassion, relaxation and step-up.


      Self care includes being responsible to reach out for professional help. So do not hesitate to seek help to help yourself. You may also want to explore these books:

      Psychological well being

      Positive and negative emotions as well as their balance are only one of the approaches to gauge our well being and it is often referred to as emotional well being.


      Another approach to understand well being is to explore how we are functioning psychologically rather than how we are feeling. In this approach, feelings are given secondary importance and seen as a by-product of how we are leading our lives.


      There are different dimensions of psychological well being.


      Let us focus on four of these components here, which are captured through psychological well being questionnaire in this wellness app.


    The psychological well being questionnaire is merely to help you discover and decide for yourself, which dimensions or aspects you might want to focus upon, in order to experience an overall increase in your well being. These dimensions also provide you the idea that you could enhance your well being in multiple ways.

    LEARNING ABOUT DIFFERENT TYPES OF ERRORS IN THINKING
    (COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS)?

    Heard of this insightful statement by William James?
    The greatest weapon against stress is our ability to choose one thought over another


    Becoming aware of our patterns of thinking, questioning and modifying them when needed can be a powerful approach to enhancing well being. In fact, the most well-researched and practiced interventions ( cognitive behavior therapies) for common mental health problems revolve around this approach and utilize multiple strategies that focus on bringing about changes in thinking patterns and beliefs thereby influencing feelings and behaviors. In this context, it becomes useful to look at the concept of cognitive distortions. Cognitive distortions are systematic errors in thinking or biases when interpreting situations, events and/or experiences. As a result of these "thinking errors" we make conclusions, or respond through our behaviors and emotions in ways that are unhelpful or dysfunctional.

    Let us become familiar with common errors in thinking so that we can spot them through self monitoring and practice.


    Dealing with cognitive errors

    Becoming aware and taking a pause to reflect on our own thoughts is the critical step in correcting cognitive errors. We can ask ourselves a few questions that could help us to dealing with cognitive errors.


    Examples of questions we can ask ourselves:

    Have I had any experiences that show that this thought is not completely true ALL the time?

    If my best friend or someone I loved had this thought, what would I tell them?

    If my best friend or someone who loves me knew I was thinking this, what would they say to me? What evidence would they point out to me that would suggest that my thoughts were not 100% true?

    Five years from now, if I look back at this situation, will I look at it any differently?

    When I am not feeling this way, do I think about this type of situation any differently?

    Are there any strengths or positives in me or the situation that I am ignoring?

    What are the advantages and disadvantages of thinking this way?

    Tip: Questioning your negative thoughts using one or more of the above questions may help in becoming aware of the fallacies in our thinking and thereby give us a scope to correct thinking errors which in turn can positively influence our moods and behaviors.

    You may want to explore: Greenberger, D., & Padesky, C. A. (1995). Mind over mood. New York: Guilford Press.

    The most powerful relationship you will ever have is the relationship with yourself

      • Busting myths about self-compassion

      We are introducing self-compassion because it has been related to well being and high self-criticality is often linked to depression and other common mental health problems. You may find this notion of self-compassion to be vague/funny/rather strange. So we will try to clear your doubts. What are the questions that come to your mind when you think about self-compassion?


      What is self-compassion?

      Compassion means being aware of/acknowledging suffering, experiencing feelings of kindness/care, and understanding that imperfections and fragility are universal aspects of being human- that all of us share in common. Self-compassion is nothing but compassion toward one's own self.


      I think what I need is to do things to maintain high self-esteem.

      Self-esteem is about a positive evaluation of oneself
      Very often, people understand and base their self- esteem on certain conditions such as being better than others in some respect, achieving things in life etc.
      Such things become the conditions for valuing ourselves positively and then our self-esteem keeps fluctuating based on whether we are able to meet these standards. This approach is not a healthy way of functioning.
      Conditional self-esteem (when we set conditions to feel Ok /good about ourselves) can enhance risk of depression in the face of failures/losses and result in undue anxiety and stress while trying to meet certain standards 'before we value ourselves'. It can also result in various undesirable methods people might use to preserve their self-esteem (e.g. putting others down, avoiding to think about what is going wrong). Self-compassion is also about positive regard for oneself but it is not a conditional state.

      Self-compassion seems like self-pity. I would not want that.

      True self-compassion is very different from self-pity. In self-pity, one focuses on oneself and may brood /ruminate over and again about the negative aspects of a situation.
      Self-compassion on the other hand is about just being aware (mindful) of the situation/feelings, rather than constantly dwelling/drowning in these.
      Moreover self-compassion draws upon the belief that suffering is universal aspect of living life for everyone so one is not special as compared to others.
      This is unlike self-pity which tends to evoke a sense of 'me' suffering in isolation from others.


      What is self-criticism?

      Self-criticism refers to internal thoughts/views about oneself, one's behaviors in a negative fashion, like fault- finding. It can be in the form of negative, comparisons of one's own self with others or internal - in terms of feeling that one has not lived up to one's own internal goals/standards.


      I think being self-critical is good. It can motivate us.

      Of course, it is important that we recognize our mistakes/errors/failures and learn from them or correct our patterns of thinking/action.
      However, this is not same as being self-critical.
      High levels of self-criticality (comparing unfairly with others or with our own harsh and unrealistic internal standards) has been linked to various kinds of mental health problems. This is because high and relentless self-criticality results in excessive brooding and worsening of moods which in turn affect our functioning.
      Although some level of anxiety and tension can motivate us to do better, over- generalized/global judgments of ourselves as persons (e.g. when I forgot to address someone appropriately during a meeting, telling myself that I am such a 'stupid person' who can 'never succeed'..) can worsen our mood to an extent that we can end up doing self-destructive things to feel better (e.g. using alcohol to cope with the resultant low mood) or avoid preparing for another upcoming meeting due to heightened sense of pressure.
      On the other hand, being self-compassionate can help us to acknowledge (rather than deny/avoid) what went wrong without unfavorably judging ourselves as 'bad /wrong persons' and thus free us to work towards improving what we want to improve.


      How is self-criticism related to being criticized by others?

      There are times when another person's comments become upsetting because they reflect what you have already been telling yourself- in the form of an internal dialogue. Thus, others' comments/behaviors may trigger negative, self-critical or self-blaming thoughts leading to sadness.


      What can I do to become less self-critical and more self-compassionate?

      Some people find it useful to look back at their past experiences to figure out why and how they learnt to become so self-critical of themselves. This may help in understanding to some extent why we carry such a harsh and critical voice within us. But such insights are not automatically sufficient to change this pattern. What is helpful is practicing the new approach repeatedly and consciously till it becomes a new habit.


      About mindfulness:

      When we are feeling disturbed, we experience negative thoughts, often about the past,/about oneself, that can further intensify the negative moods. This type of thinking, which appears to be recurring, perseveres on negative aspects is known as rumination or ruminative thinking.

      The mood worsens further when we attach significance to these thoughts (meaning of these thoughts and seeing thoughts as facts for example: a person having negative automatic thoughts of labeling - 'I am a failure', does not see it as a mere thought, instead believes that he/she is a failure and feels more distressed.)

      Mindfulness is a term that is a rough English translation of the Pali (Buddhist Tradition) word Vipassana which means 'to view in a special way'. It involves paying attention to experiences (emotions, thoughts etc.) in a special way-seeing them as mental events but non-judgmentally, with curiosity, openness and acceptance. It also involves viewing self in a non-judgmental manner, staying with whatever comes, in a non-reactive manner.

      Mindfulness can help the person become aware of and recognize unhelpful patterns of thinking, and learn to- not respond to these thoughts as if they were facts ('thoughts are not facts and I am not my thoughts!'), thereby being objective and learning to view these as passing mental phenomenon.

      Mindfulness can thus allow people to recognize mental traps or habitual patterns of thinking and to step back from them.

      You can explore mindfulness exercises online or with the help of a psychologist trained in mindfulness.

    RELAXATION THROUGH VISUALIZATION


      • Anxiety is a common mental health symptom and anxiety disorders are one of the common mental health disorders apart from depression.


      Anxiety symptoms, in general, can include one or more of the following:

      1. An experience of tension, apprehension. 2. Frequent worries regarding negative future happenings.
      3. Muscular tension, and aches and pains (such as headaches).
      4. A sense of uneasiness and difficulty in staying still/calm or in being relaxed.
      5. Bodily arousal symptoms such as breathlessness, palpitations (increased heart rate), feeling hot, sweating and a funny sensation in the stomach (sometimes described as having butterflies in the stomach).


      Although there are different manifestations or expressions of anxiety as well as different types of anxiety disorders, a common feature across all of them is the experience of physiological arousal. There are specific psychological interventions related to the nature of anxiety (e.g. generalized constant anxiety vs. brief lasting periods of acute anxiety). However, most presentations of anxiety and especially bodily arousal symptoms can be reduced by learning and applying a relaxation skill or technique. Learning how to relax is thus a skill that can be acquired to manage anxiety.


      There are muscle relaxation procedures which are best learnt under the guidance of a supervisor/counselor. Presented here are a few visualization--based approaches to relaxation. You may choose to try out and practice one of these, based on your preference, the type of anxiety you are experiencing as well as how useful you find it to be for anxiety reduction. Each of these requires regular practice in order to be effective. They must be learned sufficiently well so that they can be applied well in times of need. Remember all methods of relaxation are aimed at reducing the bodily arousal, increasing your body's as well as your mind's ability to withstand triggers to anxiety and to help you come back to a relaxed state in a short period.


      IMPORTANT NOTE:Learning to relax is not sufficient in itself for management of all anxiety symptoms and people with anxiety disorders often require a multiple set of strategies that depend on the nature of their problem. So, it is strongly recommended that you seek a professional consultation if you think you may be experiencing significant anxiety.


      Anxiety symptoms often co-occur with depressive symptoms and relaxation methods can be used as supplement in such instances. BUT, Individuals who are experiencing high severity of depressive symptoms (such as frequent crying spells, thoughts of self-harm, hopelessness, low energy, preferring to be alone, reduced social interaction) and those experiencing minimal/no anxiety or bodily arousal symptoms are unlikely to benefit from applying relaxation methods. if this is so, we recommend you to work on increasing your activity - levels using behavioral-activation and other techniques such as modifying unhelpful thoughts, rather than practicing relaxation when you are depressed. At times, certain relaxation methods may even worsen a person's mental health condition if these are not suitable at a given point of time for that person. In case you are unsure, it is best to contact a mental health professional.


      There are many different types of methods that produce a state of relaxation and calmness. Each of these methods is useful for different types or manifestations of anxiety. Two of the following methods focus on using imagery/visualization for relaxation. Pleasant imagery/rich visualization involving different senses can help absorb our attention on soothing and calming cues which can induce a relaxed state.


      You can use one of the two video clips presented here for imagery based relaxation exercise. The first exercise involves visualizing a nature scene in detail. The audio instruction can guide you to create vivid and specific images and stay focused on the same. This exercise may also be useful for entering a relaxed state just before going to sleep. The content of this exercise is based on the understanding that many people find scenes from nature to be calming and pleasant. The second exercise also utilizes our power of visualization. However, its content focuses on recreating an imagery based on spiritual experiences. The content is general and not specific to any particular religion. Those who are religiously inclined may find it to be a useful tool for relaxation.


      If neither of the visualization exercises suits your needs and preferences, you can try to create your own personalized audio-tape and use that for relaxation. Please remember that you need to utilize specific and vivid, clear images that absorb your attention and have a calming effect on you.

    DEALING WITH SUICIDAL THOUGHTS

    Here is a test to find out whether your mission in life is complete.
    If you're alive, it isn't. -Richard Bach

    Raj looked at his watch again...for the 10th time....he had been waiting for a job offer letter desperately. On top of that, after a big fight with his mother, he suddenly felt as if nothing in this world made sense anymore. He felt as if all his strength to fight with adversities in life was draining out. For a split second, a thought crossed his mind ....I might as well end it all.'


    Thoughts about ending one's life can come to anyone. There are times in our lives, when we feel stuck, and there seems to be no possible solution which can take us out of our misery. During such moments, thoughts about death and dying may cross ones mind. Thoughts about suicide can be scary. It is important to realize that getting suicidal thoughts is a sign that you are finding it difficult to cope with challenges in your life at this point of time and many of us go through such moments/periods in our lives.


    When do these thoughts occur?

    Suicidal thoughts or death-wishes can occur when we feel that we dont have enough coping resources to deal with the life-challenges we are experiencing. This may happen because of limitations in our resources (e.g. internal resources such as energy level or external resources such as supportive-figures) at any given point of time or the severity of the challenges /demands we are facing or a combination of both. Suicidal thinking can vary in severity. While death-wishes are passive thoughts where a person experiences transient wishes that life comes to an end by itself (for e.g. death due to a fatal illness, accident, during sleep etc.); active suicidal thoughts are the thoughts which involve thinking about taking ones life.


    These thoughts may cross ones mind because of various reasons such as:


    Feeling overwhelmed and unable to control situations in life (For e.g. frequent fights with friends or family)

    Feeling guilty (For e.g. blaming oneself for losing loved one)

    Feeling stuck and powerless (For e.g. feeling trapped and not seeing a way out of a difficult situation, failing in an important exam)

    Feeling angry (For e.g. thinking of suicide as a way of taking revenge)

    Feeling meaningless (For e.g. an elderly person feeling empty and seeing no purpose to live on, feeling that the family will be better off without him/her)


    Sometimes these thoughts can occur when we are feeling extremely sad, hopeless and discouraged in life.


    Although a wish to die or end it all can be part and parcel of clinical depression, such thoughts can occur independently of depression too. Moreover all individuals with depression do not necessarily experience suicidal thoughts.


    While for some people, these thoughts are fleeting and come to ones mind only in an extremely overwhelming situation; for some others, these thoughts can be intense and nagging, and may seem like the only possible solution to a problem.


    The frequency and intensity of these thoughts may vary from individual to individual, and may also change from one point of time to another in the same individual, depending on the immediate life context and how we perceive our resources to cope with difficulties. Just because you get these thoughts does not always mean that you will lose control and act on them. When suicidal thoughts cross our minds, typically it is not because we want to end our life but because we want a way to decrease our pain or suffering or to feel more in control. Hence when we are able to do something about this (even a little thing) /get even a small additional resource or see the whole issue slightly differently, it can minimize our suffering and bring back a sense of some control. Then such thoughts can decrease significantly and disappear.

    STEP UP: WHEN YOU NEED TO

    An obstacle is often a stepping stone. Prescott

    Some ups and downs in our mood and functioning, etc. are common aspects of human life and are manageable with use of self-care strategies as well as support from our loved ones. But sometimes, these disturbances in moods and functioning can indicate the need for reaching out to a mental health professional. In fact mental health problems are commoner than what most people might imagine. If there is a possibility of a mental health problem, it would help to go for an evaluation to clarify the same and to seek interventions as may be required. For most mental health conditions, it is not the lack of effective treatment but the mental and social barriers to seeking professional help that become a major obstacle to leading a fulfilling life. Informal support and professional support are not substitutes/replacements for each other. When you seek professional help, it does need to imply that self-help and informal support are irrelevant; all these can supplement and complement each other.


    When to Step-up?

    Please read and think as to which of the following statements are true for you.


    1. Did the wellness check survey indicate that you are experiencing high distress or negative emotions and low positivity ratio?

    2. Are you finding that your motivation level for engaging in your usual activities is going down quite a bit?

    3. Is the severity of your negative mood increasing /not decreasing in the last few weeks?

    4. Are the thoughts about suicide coming to your mind in the last few days/weeks?

    5. Would you say that you do not seem to be getting any benefit from using self-help methods and /or seeking support from your loved ones, despite sufficiently trying and finding that your mental health condition is worsening?

    6. Do you find that you just cannot motivate yourself to do things to help yourself, although you are aware as to what might help?

    7. Do you find that your day-to-day functioning is declining in the last few days/weeks due to your mental state?


    We advise you to seek face to face consultation with a trained mental health professional if you notice that any one or more of the above statements is true for you.


    Overcoming mental blocks to seeking professional help

    It is very common for people to experience mental barriers/blocks to seek help from a mental health professional, even if they need such help and even if they know that it may be of some use.

    This may be due to a variety of factors such as sense of stigma/fear of being stigmatized, fear of the unknown, doubts about oneself etc.

    As a first step, you can ask us and clarify your doubts regarding seeking direct professional help.
